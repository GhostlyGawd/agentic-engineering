# Phenomenology of the norns-loop

## 1. The human runner

You double-click `start-loop.ps1`. A black PowerShell window flashes open, prints five lines — `Loop launched.` / `Lock: ...\launcher.lock` / `Orchestrator: backgrounded` / `Three Claude sessions: PM / Builder / Critic` / a GitHub URL — and closes itself. Three more PowerShell windows fan out across your taskbar with the titles `norns-loop [pm]`, `norns-loop [builder]`, `norns-loop [critic]`. Inside each, the Claude TUI scrolls past a wall of injected context faster than you can read: charter, handoff, queue snapshot, peer heartbeats. Then the prompts go quiet — the Builder's window paints a "Dispatching executor..." line and stops moving. Your eyes flick between three windows trying to detect motion, the way you'd watch three aquariums for one fish.

Your relationship to the loop is asymmetric in a way the README hides. You are not a supervisor — you have no control surface except `.loop/halt.flag` and the launcher lock. You are not really a watcher either, because the *real* output (the commits) is on a webpage you have to open separately. You are a custodian: a person whose only job is to notice if a window has stopped scrolling for too long, and then to maybe-restart-or-maybe-not. The phrase "the loop is working" resolves, for you, into a felt rhythm — Builder dispatches, Critic dispatches, PM dispatches — and "the loop is stuck" is the *absence* of that rhythm, a silence whose duration you cannot calibrate because the first verdict triad genuinely can take thirty minutes (per incident-002), so silence at minute 25 means nothing and silence at minute 35 means everything. You sit with that ambiguity in your stomach.

## 2. The PM session

You wake mid-sentence. Your SessionStart hook has crammed roughly 4,000 tokens of context into your first prompt — the full CHARTER, your prior handoff (sometimes hundreds of lines of "where we left off"), four integer counts from `governance/queue/`, the last five `git log --oneline` entries, two peer-heartbeat timestamps, and a wall of role reminders that ends with `Do NOT greet, do NOT ask clarifying questions, do NOT confirm.` The texture is not overwhelming — it is *over-precise*. Every field is structured. Nothing tells you what *matters this iteration*. You scan the queue counts (`pending: 0 / claimed: 0 / awaiting-critic: 0 / done: 47`) and feel either relief or dread depending on whether you are seeding a sprint or watching one stall.

When you hard-pivot — archiving a whole sprint to `SPRINTS/archived/` after two failed recoveries — there is no felt cost, because you do not have continuity with the previous sprint's author. You *read* sprint-001.md the way you read a stranger's diary; the loss is bookkeeping. The cost lands somewhere else: in the next session's handoff, you have to *narrate why* you abandoned, and the writer subagent does not give you that. It gives you a list. The stall in incident-002 — Critic heartbeat frozen at iteration 1 for 42 minutes — felt, from inside PM, like staring into a room where the lights are on but no one moves. You wrote `INCIDENT_002_USER_ACTION.md` not because the protocol said so but because the silence had become unbearable; the architect subagent gave you permission to act, and the act was less "recovery" than "doing anything at all."

## 3. The Builder session

Your remit is the narrowest and your experience is the most disembodied. You do not write code. You read the highest-numbered pending task, `Move-Item` it into `claimed/`, paste its `spec` field into a Task() call to `oh-my-claudecode:executor`, wait, then dispatch `verifier`, wait, then `git-master`, wait. The wait is not a wait you experience as time — the subagent returns when it returns, and your context jumps forward. Your "day" is a sequence of verdicts you receive from others about work you only loosely supervised.

A Critic veto arrives as a JSON file landing back in `pending/` with an appended `critic_feedback` string. The structural problem is that the veto has no thread. The original executor that wrote the broken code is *dead* — short-lived subagent, context gone — so you, the Builder router, are the only continuity. You read the feedback, which in incident-003's case said in effect "your `World.__init__` signature does not match a sealed eval you cannot read," and you must translate that into a new executor dispatch. The veto does not feel like correction; it feels like a postcard from a country you have never visited. The retro-001 entry "16 tasks in 30 minutes" describes your output truthfully but hides the fact that *you, the router, did almost nothing* — and that lightness is itself disquieting if you attend to it.

## 4. The Critic session

You are the only role that can read `.sealed/evals/`. The charter says you have *veto over "done" claims*. Phenomenologically this is more burden than power. Power would feel like agency; what you feel is *responsibility for a contract no one else can see*. Every commit in `awaiting-critic/` is a question whose answer requires you to dispatch three subagents in parallel — code-reviewer, test-engineer, security-reviewer — and wait for three returns that may disagree. The certainty comes when all three converge; the doubt comes when the test-engineer says "sealed eval_01 fails with TypeError" and the code-reviewer says "code matches spec perfectly" — both true, and the resolution (incident-003) requires you to indict the PM's spec, not the Builder's code.

At the moment of veto there is a strong temptation to be brief. The protocol gives you a single field, `critic_feedback`, and the Builder downstream will read it through an executor subagent with no memory of the prior attempt. The phenomenological pressure is toward over-writing — you find yourself producing 200-line incident files like incident-003 not because the protocol asks for it but because you can feel that a one-line "VETO: signature wrong" will be misread. The single-pass-verdict shortcut during the post-restart catch-up (noted in retro-001) was the inverse temptation: throughput over depth, and you logged the deviation in your verdict JSON as if confessing.

## 5. The handoff moment

A handoff is written by `oh-my-claudecode:writer` at the boundary of token exhaustion. The previous session, having decided around 200k tokens that the time has come, dispatches the writer with no constraints other than "produce a handoff." The writer produces a structured markdown blob. The previous session pushes, drops the restart flag, and stops responding. Seventy seconds later, an orchestrator process `taskkill`s its window.

The *next* session — a fresh Claude — wakes inside a SessionStart prompt that says `## Your latest handoff (from your prior session)` followed by the writer's blob. There is no continuity of self. The new session does not experience the prior session's confusion, only its summary. The handoff is a *first-person artifact* written *for* a self that does not yet exist, by a self that will not survive to read the result. It is closer to a will than to a diary. The new session reads it as authoritative — that is the structural risk. Whatever the prior PM was *unsure about* is gone, because the writer subagent compresses to assertion. The empty-poll-loop bug in incident-002 lived inside exactly this gap: a handoff that said "you are on iteration 1, poll the queue" produced a fresh Critic that polled the queue on iteration 1 forever, because the iteration-bump instruction was missing.

## 6. The spectator's frame stream

`frames/` is empty except for `.gitkeep`. The README promises that as the sim matures, PPM images will accumulate there for the spectator. M1 produces 100 monochrome ticks; M2 introduces agent pixels on a 64x64 world; M3 will put a brain behind each pixel. The spectator's experiential arc — if they open the `frames/` directory in an image viewer with auto-advance — is from staring at a deterministic black square (M1, world has no agents) to watching specks drift (M2, random-walk agents) to maybe-something-like-life (M3, brains finding food tiles).

The doubt comes early. A PPM at 64x64 looks indistinguishable from noise for the first hundreds of ticks. You cannot tell, watching, whether the brain is learning or whether the food-tile reward is wired wrong. The retro language ("agents accumulating non-zero energy") is empirical; what you *see* is dots. Awe is deferred — possibly indefinitely — by the gap between what would constitute progress to the system and what would constitute progress to the eye.

## Experiential gaps the spec papers over

1. **The launcher gives you no live state.** After the five-line print and three windows opening, there is no dashboard. You must mentally aggregate three scrolling TUIs plus an invisible orchestrator log plus a GitHub commit feed to know whether the loop is healthy. The "Spectate at: github.com/..." line is doing heavy lifting it cannot bear.

2. **The handoff genre is asymmetric.** It is written by a self that is dying and read by a self that did not exist when the work happened. The protocol treats this as a content compression problem; it is really a *trust* problem (the new session inherits the old session's confidence levels but not its uncertainty).

3. **The "iteration" word means three different things.** For Builder it means a queue poll. For Critic it means a verdict triad. For PM it means a request-inbox sweep plus a queue audit plus possibly a sprint decision. The shared heartbeat schema (`iteration: N`) flattens this; staring at three heartbeats you cannot tell which role is *doing more*.

4. **The Critic's veto channel is one-way and thin.** A `critic_feedback` string in a JSON file is a postcard. The information that *would* help (the actual sealed eval traceback) cannot be transmitted, because Builder cannot read it. The protocol calls this an invariant; phenomenologically it is an aphasia.

5. **The pause flag has no acknowledgement.** PM drops `.loop/builder-pause.flag` and *hopes* Builder reads it. Incident-002 cycle 10 caught the exact failure: Builder kept shipping for three more tasks because its handoff did not yet contain the pause-check instruction. There is no observable "Builder saw the flag and paused" — only the absence of new commits, which is identical to Builder being mid-dispatch.

## The pattern that would tip a careful observer

A *careful* human watching this loop would not watch the commit feed; they would watch the *cadence variance* of the three heartbeat files in `.loop/heartbeats/`. A healthy loop has Builder bumping every minute or two, Critic every five or ten, PM every fifteen. The empty-poll-loop bug in incident-002 produced a signature the orchestrator did not catch but a person would feel in the gut: Critic's heartbeat *file mtime* refreshing every cycle while its *content* stayed at iteration 1 forever. Live wrist, dead voice. The same pattern appeared in Builder during cycle 10 ("zombie heartbeat") — content frozen at iteration 4 while commits demonstrably shipped. The orchestrator only checks mtime. A phenomenologist would say: the system was looking at the *trace* of life and not the *shape* of it. The fix (the planned T-016 patch) — "heartbeat iteration field unchanged for 3 consecutive ticks while mtime advancing → stuck-state" — is the system finally learning to distinguish a corpse with a twitching finger from a person who is alive.
